Introduction to SPSS, ANOVA, and Contrast testing

Author

Michael Woller

Introduction

In this lesson, there are three primary objectives: (1) to introduce you to running ANOVAs in SPSS using syntax, (2) to look at the similarities between the different procedures one can use in SPSS for conducting ANOVA, and (3) introduce you to contrasts and how to formulate them for hypothesis testing.

We will test group differences among three groups using three SPSS procedures: one-way ANOVA, univariate ANOVA, and multivariate ANOVA (MANOVA). Note, SPSS can’t be ran natively in Quarto (how I am generating this page), so all results I am copy/pasting from SPSS output.

The Dataset

The data we are using today comes from a study that tested a phenomenon known as stereotype threat. Aronson et al. (1998) describe this phenomenon as when “members of the stereotyped group often feel pressure in situations where their behavior can confirm the negative reputation that their group lacks a valued ability.” One such stereotype is that females are worse at math than males. Females in our study were assigned to take a math test in one of three conditions:

  1. A control group.
  2. A condition in which they were “reminded” prior to testing of the belief that males perform better.
  3. A condition in which they were told to read a report that refutes the stereotype prior to testing.

Stereotype threat would be evidenced if females performed worse when told that males outperform them. Here are the scores from the three groups:

1. General SPSS Notes

Before running any analyses, a few things to know about SPSS syntax.

Procedures - SPSS is broken into explicit procedures, which can comprise multiple lines of code. Each procedure begins differently, but they all end with a period. That is, a period tells SPSS that you are done describing a procedure and that SPSS should use your input to run it. Think of it like a sentence. Do not forget to end each procedure with a period, or SPSS will wait for more input. Many statistical analysis software are like this, so you may be familiar with the concept.

Comments - Comments in SPSS are plain-language parts of your syntax that are totally ignored by the computer. While comments in software like R or Python begin with #, in SPSS everything after /* is a comment. Anything denoted as a comment will not be read as syntax code. If you want to do a procedure as a comment, you start a line with * and end it with a period before moving onto actionable syntax.

Case - Syntax in SPSS does not have to be all caps. It can be lowercase too. However, much of what we will do is in all caps, since it makes it easier to distinguish between parts of the code.

General structure - In general, every procedure will follow this pattern:

STATE WHAT PROCEDURE YOU ARE USING
STATE YOUR MODEL
/ANY FURTHER OPTIONS WILL FOLLOW THE "/" SYMBOL IN THIS NEXT LINE.

2. ANOVA: Partitioning Variability

ANOVA partitions the total variability in an outcome (\(SS_{total}\)) into two parts: variability explained by the group factor (\(SS_{between}\)) and variability left unexplained (\(SS_{within}\), also called \(SS_{error}\)).

\[SS_{total} = SS_{between} + SS_{within}\]

The key idea is that full models contain all effects (the group mean effect), and reduced models contain no effects. The error of the full model (\(E_F\)) is \(SS_{within}\), and the error of the reduced model (\(E_R\)) is \(SS_{total}\). Their difference gives us \(SS_{between}\).

\[E_R - E_F = SS_{effect} = SS_{between}\]

Basically, the idea is that the effects of interest are the ones we do NOT include in the model. By knowing all the variability in the data when there are no effects accounting for them (the total variability), and seeing how much of that is reduced when we do account for the effects (the full model), we can see how important individual effects are.

The F Test

The mean square (MS) quantifies the sizes of each partition of variability. The general omnibus F test finds the ratio between the two partitions:

\[F = rac{(E_R - E_F)/(df_R - df_F)}{E_F/df_F} = rac{SS_{Between}/df_{Between}}{SS_{Within}/df_{Within}} = rac{MS_{Between}}{MS_{Within}}\]

Where the degrees of freedom are:

  • \(df_{total}\) = \(N - 1\)
  • \(df_{within}\) = \(N - a\)
  • \(df_{between}\) = \(a - 1\)

To find a critical F value in R: qf(1 - ALPHA, df_between, df_within) or qf(ALPHA, df_between, df_within, lower.tail = FALSE). You can also use the online calculator at https://www.danielsoper.com/statcalc/calculator.aspx?id=4.

3. One-Way ANOVA with ONEWAY

The ONEWAY procedure in SPSS runs a one-way between-subjects ANOVA. The general syntax structure is:

ONEWAY          /* The procedure being used (a one-way ANOVA)
DV BY IV        /* This line describes the model: Y (DV) predicted by X (IV)
/STATISTICS DESCRIPTIVES.    /* Creates a table of descriptive statistics

For our stereotype threat data, where Math is the outcome and Group is the grouping variable:

ONEWAY              /* The procedure being used
MATH BY GROUP       /* Math (DV) predicted by Group (IV)
/STATISTICS DESCRIPTIVES.    /* Creates a table of descriptive statistics

4. Univariate ANOVA with UNIANOVA

The UNIANOVA procedure runs a General Linear Model using a univariate approach. This is only appropriate for between-subjects designs. The syntax is very similar to ONEWAY but uses a /DESIGN line to explicitly identify the grouping variable as the independent variable.

UNIANOVA          /* The procedure being used (univariate ANOVA)
DV BY IV          /* Same as what is done above
/DESIGN = IV.     /* DESIGN identifies the grouping variable as the IV

For our data:

UNIANOVA           /* The procedure being used
MATH BY GROUP      /* Same as for ONEWAY
/DESIGN = GROUP.   /* Identifies the grouping variable

5. MANOVA

The MANOVA procedure (Multivariate Analysis of Variance) is the most flexible of the three. It can be done for within-subjects, between-subjects, one-way, two-way, split plot, and other designs. We will use MANOVA for the majority of analyses this semester because of its flexibility.

The general syntax is:

/* Multivariate ANOVA for Y (DV) by X (IV)
/* Replace "min" and "max" with the grouping values (smallest to largest)

MANOVA DV BY IV (min max)
/PRINT CELLINFO(ALL)   /* Print descriptive information for each cell in the design
/DESIGN.               /* Specifies the effects of interest within the specified model
                       /* Default is a full factorial model

For our data:

MANOVA MATH BY GROUP (1 3)       /* DV "by" grouping var; ( ) enclose min and max grouping values
/PRINT CELLINFO(ALL)             /* Print descriptive information for each cell
/DESIGN.                         /* Specifies the effects of interest

6. Contrasts

What Are Contrasts?

Typically, we are not only interested in the overall omnibus F test, but also in the comparison of individual means and combinations of means through pairwise and complex comparisons. Contrasts help test specific, custom hypotheses of mean differences between groups instead of the generic omnibus hypothesis. Contrasts are denoted as \(\psi\).

As seen in Lecture 2, contrasts let us manipulate the SS partitions into further sub-partitions.

Orthogonality

Contrasts must be orthogonal. There are three requirements:

1. The sum of each contrast’s coefficients must equal 0: \[c_1 + c_2 + \ldots = 0\]

2. We need linear independence - the positive coefficients should sum to 1, and the negative coefficients should sum to -1. This prevents a mean from appearing on both sides of the hypothesis.

Note: they do not actually have to equal exactly 1 or -1 so long as they are in correct proportions. For example, you cannot denote repeating decimals in SPSS (e.g., 1/3 = .333333…), so you have to multiply the fractions by their denominator:

\[\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} - 1 \rightarrow 1 + 1 + 1 - 3\]

These are equivalent. However, if you rescale your contrast coefficients this way, you must divide the confidence interval endpoints by the same denominator to get back to the proper scale. E.g., if you used 1 1 1 -3, divide the ends of your confidence interval by 3.

3. The sum of each product of contrasts must be 0: \[\sum c_{1j} c_{2j} = 0\]

For example, given \(\psi_1 = 1\mu_1 - 1\mu_2 - 1\mu_3 + 1\mu_4\) and \(\psi_2 = \frac{1}{3}\mu_1 + \frac{1}{3}\mu_2 + \frac{1}{3}\mu_3 - 1\mu_4\):

\[\Sigma c_1 c_2 = 1 \cdot \frac{1}{3} + (-1) \cdot \frac{1}{3} + (-1) \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 1 \cdot (-1) = 0\]

Repeat this check for each combination of contrasts (\(\psi_1 \times \psi_3\), \(\psi_2 \times \psi_3\), etc.).

Groups of Contrasts

When testing a special matrix of contrast coefficients you also need to follow these rules:

  • You cannot test more than \(a - 1\) contrasts, where \(a\) is the number of groups. One row must be all 1’s (representing the grand mean in the structural equation) - this does not count toward your \(a - 1\).
  • The matrix must be square (\(a \times a\)).

Contrast Partitioning

When contrasts are orthogonal, \(\Sigma SS_{contrast} = SS_{between}\) - the contrasts exactly partition the between-groups variability. If contrasts cover too little, \(\Sigma SS_{contrast} < SS_{between}\). If they cover too much (non-orthogonal), \(\Sigma SS_{contrast} > SS_{between}\).

7. Contrasts within ONEWAY

The ONEWAY procedure allows you to specify contrasts directly using /contrast= lines. Each /contrast= line specifies the coefficients for one contrast.

ONEWAY pressure BY treat
/statistics descriptives
/contrast= 1 1 1 -3
/contrast= 1 -1 0 0
/contrast= 2 -2 0 0.

Note that the contrast 2 -2 0 0 gives the same test as 1 -1 0 0 - they differ only in scaling.

[Screenshot of ONEWAY contrast output goes here]

Important limitation: This syntax works well for one-way between-subjects designs but fails for other designs (within-subjects, multiple factors, multiple dependent variables, etc.). The MANOVA procedure is much more flexible, so we will use it for the majority of analyses this semester. In fact, ONEWAY can do some fundamental types of analyses that cannot be done easily in either SAS or R, which is one reason this class focuses on SPSS.

8. Contrasts within MANOVA

To run contrasts in MANOVA, we add a special matrix that identifies what contrasts we want to test.

MANOVA pressure BY treat (1 4)
/print=cellinfo(means)
/error=within
/contrast(treat) = special (1 1 1 1
                             1 1 1 -3
                             0.5 0.5 -0.5 -0.5
                             1 -1 0 0)
/design = treat(1) treat(2) treat(3).

Here is what each line does:

Line 1 specifies your dependent variable (pressure) and your independent grouping variable (treat). The numbers in the parentheses represent the range of groups in the IV (group 1 through 4).

Line 2 tells SPSS to print out the means of each group. This line is not necessary, but omitting it means you will not have the group means conveniently provided to you.

Line 3 specifies the type of SS error we are using for the ANOVA. We will use the within-error option. This line is redundant since it is the default, but include it for transparency.

Line 4, the special matrix, identifies the contrasts we are interested in testing. Guidelines for the special matrix:

  1. The first row consists of all ones. This adds the grand mean to our model (equivalently, the intercept in the regression framework). Without it, SPSS would not know what baseline to use for your contrasts.

  2. Each of the following rows must sum to zero. This adequately constructs your contrast to represent tests of group mean comparisons. If you are using an odd number of groups (e.g., 3 groups vs 1), you need to write the contrasts in whole numbers since you cannot represent repeating decimals. Remember to rescale your confidence intervals if you do this.

  3. The special matrix must always be a square matrix, with the number of columns and rows equal to \(a\), the number of groups. You can test \(a - 1\) contrasts per special matrix, though you can run multiple MANOVAs to test more than \(a - 1\) contrasts.

  4. Contrasts cannot be linearly dependent. This is usually done by making contrasts orthogonal. For between-subjects balanced designs (equal group sample sizes), contrasts are automatically statistically independent of each other even if not orthogonal. For within-subjects or unbalanced designs, orthogonality is required. Check orthogonality by computing the dot product \(\Sigma_k C_{i,k} \cdot C_{j,k}\) for each pair of contrasts.

  5. To avoid confusion, specify that the positive coefficients sum to 1 and the negative coefficients sum to -1.

Line 5, the /design line, specifies the tests we are performing. treat(1) means: test the DV with the grouping variable treat using the first contrast (after the line of 1’s). Include all contrasts in the design line even if you are only interested in one - SPSS gives incorrect results if you do not. You always have to include this line if your analysis is an unbalanced design.

Testing More Than \(a - 1\) Contrasts

If you want to test more than \(a - 1\) contrasts, call on the MANOVA command more than once with a separate special matrix in each. The above rules must be upheld for both matrices. The bolded contrasts below are the ones of interest; the non-bolded contrasts are necessary to include to make the matrix linearly independent.

MANOVA pressure BY treat (1 4)
/print=cellinfo(means)
/error=within
/contrast(treat) = special (1  1    1    1
                             1  1    1   -3
                             0.5 0.5 -0.5 -0.5
                             1  -1    0    0)
/design = treat(1) treat(2) treat(3).

MANOVA pressure BY treat (1 4)
/print=cellinfo(means)
/error=within
/contrast(treat) = special (1    1    1    1
                             0.5  0.5  0   -1
                             0    1   -1    0
                             1    1   -1   -1)
/design = treat(1) treat(2) treat(3).

Q4: Comparing the two contrasts .5 .5 -.5 -.5 and 1 1 -1 -1 in the first and second MANOVA blocks, what do you find?

Note: Unfortunately, the MANOVA procedure cannot accommodate data that do not meet the homogeneity of variance assumption, though the ONEWAY procedure can. MANOVA never uses a separate error term. The same is true of PROC GLM in SAS.
